Overview and Purpose

In late 2015, I was in undergrad at the University of Michigan-Dearborn. One of the things I was interested in from my classes at the College of Business, was using Excel for statistics and data collection. So, when I entered my first tournament, I decided I would start collecting data about my matches with no real goal in mind other than being able to mess around with Excel. This page contains some of my anecdotal results, takeaways, and findings. Many of the graphs and charts you see on this page are dynamic and will change as the data is updated, and many of the things I write will be generalizations about the data so I won't have to keep updating this. I'm also going to try and keep my explanations short, and only include the things I find interesting, as I could break this down month-by-month or year-by-year for each section.

Environment:

Google Sheets: A Google Sheets workbook with all my data and charts. Data tracked: Tournament Name, Date, Location, Venue, Match Type (eg. Pools), Opponents Tag, Opponent’s Character, My Character, Stage, Game Win/Loss, Set Win/Loss.

Google Drawings: The charts used in Sheets can be embedded in websites but the Google internals serving them don’t work well with dynamic styling. Using Google Drawings is a neat workaround. It allows you to embed charts as images using the img tag instead of the iframe tag, while still maintaining their ability to be automatically updated with changing data. If you are interested in hearing more about this workaround, I did a write-up here.

Github: Website is hosted on github since I’m mostly just using embeds, I only really need front-end.

Known Issues: Google Drive’s internals are known for causing issues with government/corporate firewalls. If you are having trouble loading some of the graphs and charts on this page at work, then that could be the cause. The other thing is that this website is hosted on Github so there's no SSL certificate and might be blocked by your browser, but if this is the case then you can’t read this anyway.

Tournament Data

If you're a part of the Melee scene you've likely heard my origin many times before. In 2015, I started my journey in competitive Super Smash Brothers Melee (2001). Since I was now mostly done with competitive sports, and in college, I had a competitive void to fill. Then, I happened to watch The Smash Brothers documentary and decided to give it a shot. My first tournament set ever was actually a doubles set with my brother against KJH and Kalamazhu, two regionally ranked players at the time. You could probably guess how that went. Definitions of Tournament Types Represented in My Graphs
Weekly A tournament series held every week in the same general area.
Local A weekend event that may be a regular event (monthly/bi-weekly), but is not big enough to be a regional.
Bar Tourney Just how it sounds, a for-fun tournament that takes place in a bar type venue.
Regional A larger weekend event that usually draws attendants from out-of-state.
Arcadian A weekend tournament where ranked players are barred from entering.
Major A large event that draws players from all over, but maybe doesn't have a large percent of the top100 players.
Super Major The largest type of event that draws players from all over and has a significant portion of the top100 players (eg. The Big House, Genesis, EVO).

Improvement

Improvement was something I was very interested in tracking since improvement in this game is more noticeable on a bi-weekly basis than any other competitive endeavor I've tried. The two most important metrics for tracking improvement that I've found are: Sets Played Per Tournament (shown in Fig. 10) and Win % Over Time (shown in Fig 12). Neither of these metrics are perfect, in fact they might only be usable due to the consistency and frequency that I went to tournaments. The reasoning behind including "Sets Played Per Tournament" as an improvement metric is that if I'm playing more sets per tournament, then I'm making it further and further in the tournament on average. This isn't always the case since not every tournament is double elimination bracket. There are many different bracket formats that all guarantee different amounts of matches played. I recommend looking mostly at the trendline overall and not really at the month-to-month variance.

It's also worth noting that I stopped entering in-person events in Feb-2020 due to COVID, but I continued to enter online tournaments without tracking the data. PGstats, a website that pulls tournament data from smash.gg has me at a 68% win rate from the period of Mar-2020 to Mar-2022, a win rate that is still roughly following the trendline.

Stage Data

Up until this point all the data was based on sets, but now we will be introducing game stats. Competitive melee is usually based on "Best of 3" or "Best of 5" sets, where each game could be played with a different character on a different stage in the game. It is now important to note that the character I play is Falco.

Figure 13 shows the percentage of my total games played on each stage. Battlefield and Yoshi's Story make up a large percentage of my games. This is because Battlefield is Falco's most common neutral starting stage against most characters. Yoshi's Story is a common starting stage against two very common characters: Fox and Captain Falcon, as well as on of Falco's most common counterpick stages.

Fig(s) 14-19 show my win percentage on each stage. As stated earlier, Yoshi's Story is typically seen as a good stage for Falco, though I'm not sure why Fountain of Dreams is so high. It might just suit my strengths as a player.

Character Data

One of the more interesting things from my data collection is the distribution of characters that I've played against. As shown in Fig 21, Fox, Falco, and Marth account for over 50% of games played. This can be used as a moderately accurate way to gauge the character distribution of the general competitive melee population. This pie chart contains every character in the game even though you can't really see most of them. There are still some characters that I've never played against in a tournament.

Figure 22 shows my win percent versus each relevant character. You can't really use this information to develop a matchup spread for Falco. Honestly this chart mostly just reflects my strong and weak matchups as a player, with some exceptions, like my Ice Climbers win percentage being skewed from one of my most played opponents being an Ice Climbers player (shown later in Fig 24).

Fig 23 shows my win percent against every character (where there was enough data) on each stage. I didn't include Yoshi because there have still been stages that I haven't played on, if that gives you an idea of what the lesser picked characters' data looks like. In matchups with less impactful counterpicks, the win percent is actually the opposite of what you would expect. If I'm the one stage counterpicking then that means I have already lost a game. So against people better than me, I'm playing more often on my counterpick stages, thus more likely to lose, and the reverse applies to my opponents counterpicks. There are some exceptions, notably Final Destination vs Peach and IC's. Keep in mind these win percentages are from Falco's perspective.

Head to Head Records

This chart (Figure 24) shows my most played opponents sorted by number of sets played. This may differ from this chart sorted by games played since in melee there is not a fixed number of games played in a set, instead, each set can be anywhere from 2 to 5 games played. The players in this chart are players that I routinely ran into at tournaments. Since certain players end up making it further in bracket consistently, it's not surprising to see most of the people on this list have a winning record against me, seeing as though I would eventually play them if I made it far enough.

Future Work

This write-up was mostly just to prove to myself that I didn’t waste all that time collecting data. Since I started doing this, smashgg has released an api allowing you to use data directly from tournaments run on their platform. This would be the eventual goal of this project if not for the fact that tournaments are still lax in recording stage and character information for tournament sets. Maybe eventually when people start recording this information I could compare my career stats with other players’.
